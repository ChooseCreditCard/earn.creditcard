How Credit Cards Earn Cashback, Points & Miles

Earn.Creditcard breaks down reward mechanics: base rates, bonus categories, multipliers, point valuation and how issuers actually pay for all of it.

Compare high-earning credit cards

What Does “Earn Rate” Really Mean?

Every card has a base earn rate — usually between 0.5% and 1.5% cashback or 1–3 points per dollar. On top of this, issuers add category bonuses and promotional multipliers.

  • Cashback: simple, fixed-value earnings.
  • Points: flexible, sometimes transferable.
  • Miles: can be high value but harder to redeem.
  • Tokens: used by some fintech reward programs.

The higher the earn rate, the more important it is to watch for annual fees and redemption limits.

Bonus Categories: Where Earn Rates Spike

Most cards give higher rewards in certain spending categories:

  • Groceries
  • Restaurants
  • Gas / transport
  • Online purchases
  • Travel
  • Streaming subscriptions

These categories are mapped to MCC codes in the Visa/Mastercard network — not merchant branding. That’s why “restaurant inside a store” may code differently than expected.

Multipliers & Limited-Time Boosts

Issuers often use multipliers to drive engagement:

  • 5× for online shopping weekends
  • 3× at grocery stores for first 3 months
  • 10% cashback on special categories
  • Rotating categories each quarter

Multipliers can be powerful — but some are capped or require activation.

How Issuers Pay for Rewards

Rewards are funded through interchange fees, partnerships (e.g. airlines), breakage (unused points) and promotional budgets.

A high reward rate is not automatically “free money” — issuers expect increased usage, loyalty and long-term profitability.

Comparing Earn Structures

Type Strengths Considerations
Flat Cashback Simple, predictable value Often capped at ~1–2%
Bonus Categories High earn rates where you spend most MCC mismatch can reduce value
Points / Miles Potentially high redemption value Complexity & devaluations
Token Rewards High advertised rates Volatility & liquidity risk
